\begin{abstract}
Zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) is one of the most widely used quantum error mitigation techniques, yet its theoretical foundations as an inference problem remain underdeveloped.
We formalize ZNE as a constrained inverse problem: recovering an unobserved noiseless expectation value from finite, noisy measurements at amplified noise levels.
We show that without function-class restrictions, the zero-noise limit is not identifiable from any finite set of observations (Proposition~\ref{prop:nonident}).
We establish that spectral bounds from the observable's eigenvalues provide physically valid constraints, and that post-hoc projection of a scalar estimate onto these bounds cannot increase pointwise error when the true value lies within the spectral interval (Proposition~\ref{prop:spectral}).
We introduce the \emph{ambiguity diameter}---the range of zero-noise values consistent with data, function class, and physical constraints---as a quantitative measure of identifiability.
Synthetic experiments illustrate that physical bounds reduce ambiguity diameter by up to $6.2\times$ in a degree-4 polynomial experiment at tolerance $\delta = 0.1$, that identifiability depends on the ratio of data points to model complexity rather than sample size alone, and that a bias--ambiguity tradeoff governs model selection for ZNE.
All numerical results are synthetic and intended to illustrate the identifiability framework, not to claim superiority of any particular estimator.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) mitigates errors on noisy quantum processors by measuring expectation values at multiple amplified noise levels and extrapolating to the zero-noise limit~\cite{LiBenjamin2017,Temme2017}.
The technique requires no knowledge of the noise model---only the ability to amplify noise controllably, typically via unitary folding~\cite{GiurgicaTiron2020}---making it one of the most accessible error mitigation strategies for near-term quantum computing.

In standard practice, ZNE is treated as a curve-fitting exercise: choose a model (linear, polynomial, exponential), fit it to noisy data, and evaluate at zero noise.
This framing obscures a fundamental question that we believe deserves formal treatment:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Under what physical and statistical conditions is the zero-noise limit identifiable from finite noisy observations?}
\end{quote}

We argue that ZNE is more naturally understood as a \textbf{constrained inverse problem} in the sense of Hadamard~\cite{Hadamard1902}: the noiseless expectation value is never directly observed, and must be inferred from indirect measurements subject to physical constraints.
This perspective reveals that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Without restricting the function class, ZNE is ill-posed---infinitely many smooth functions pass through the data but disagree at zero noise.
\item Physical constraints (spectral bounds from the observable) provide valid restrictions that reduce the set of admissible solutions.
\item The degree of identifiability depends on the interaction between function class, physical constraints, and measurement uncertainty.
\item A bias--ambiguity tradeoff governs model selection: restrictive models identify $f(0)$ tightly but may be biased; flexible models are unbiased but poorly identified.
\end{enumerate}

Recent work on QEM lower bounds~\cite{Takagi2022,Quek2024} has established that generic quantum error mitigation requires exponential sampling overhead in the worst case.
We do not claim to circumvent these bounds.
Instead, we study the mathematical structure of the ZNE inference problem to understand when and why extrapolation can be well-posed under physical constraints.

\textbf{Contributions.} We formalize ZNE as a constrained inverse problem (\cref{sec:inverse}), prove that the zero-noise limit is not identifiable without function-class assumptions (\cref{sec:nonident}), establish spectral admissibility constraints (\cref{sec:spectral}), introduce the ambiguity diameter as a quantitative identifiability metric, and present synthetic experiments illustrating degree sensitivity, tolerance sensitivity, sample-size effects, and the bias--ambiguity tradeoff (\cref{sec:experiments}).

\section{ZNE as a Constrained Inverse Problem}
\label{sec:inverse}

\subsection{Observation model}

Let $O$ be a Hermitian observable on a quantum system and $\mathcal{E}_\lambda$ a noise channel parameterized by scale factor $\lambda \geq 0$, where $\lambda = 0$ corresponds to the noiseless (ideal) channel.
The noiseless expectation is $f(0) = \mathrm{Tr}[\rho_{\mathrm{ideal}} \, O]$, and the noisy expectation at scale $\lambda$ is $f(\lambda) = \mathrm{Tr}[\mathcal{E}_\lambda(\rho_{\mathrm{ideal}}) \, O]$.

The observation model is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:obs}
y_i = f(\lambda_i) + \varepsilon_i, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n
\end{equation}
where $\varepsilon_i$ is measurement (shot) noise with variance $\sigma_i^2$.

\subsection{Fixed design points}

The scale factors $\boldsymbol{\lambda} = (\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ with $0 < \lambda_1 < \cdots < \lambda_n$ are \emph{fixed, known, exact} design points chosen by the experimenter (e.g., via unitary folding).
The target $\lambda = 0$ is never observed; it is the extrapolation target.
This makes ZNE a fixed-design regression problem with extrapolation beyond the observed domain.

\subsection{Function class}

\begin{definition}[Function class]
A function class $\mathcal{F}$ is a set of candidate response functions $f: [0, \lambda_n] \to \mathbb{R}$.
\end{definition}

Common choices include polynomials $\mathcal{P}_d$ of degree $\leq d$, single exponentials, and Lipschitz-continuous functions.
The choice of $\mathcal{F}$ is an \emph{assumption}, not a consequence of the data.

\subsection{Physical constraint set}

\begin{definition}[Physical constraint set]
For observable $O$ with eigenvalues $\{\mu_j\}$, the physical constraint set is $\mathcal{C} = [\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]$.
\end{definition}

For Pauli observables, $\mathcal{C} = [-1, 1]$; for probabilities, $\mathcal{C} = [0, 1]$.

\subsection{Ambiguity set and diameter}

\begin{definition}[Ambiguity set]
\label{def:ambiguity}
Given observations $\mathbf{y}$, function class $\mathcal{F}$, physical constraints $\mathcal{C}$, and tolerance $\delta \geq 0$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{y}, \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{C}, \delta) = \left\{ f(0) \;\middle|\; f \in \mathcal{F},\, f(0) \in \mathcal{C},\, \|f(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) - \mathbf{y}\|_\infty \leq \delta \right\}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ambiguity diameter]
$\mathrm{diam}(\mathcal{A}) = \sup \mathcal{A} - \inf \mathcal{A}$.
\end{definition}

When $\mathrm{diam}(\mathcal{A}) = 0$, $f(0)$ is identifiable.
When $\mathrm{diam}(\mathcal{A}) = \mu_{\max} - \mu_{\min}$, the constraints provide no information beyond physics.

\subsection{Condition number}

For a linear estimator $\hat{f}(0) = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{y}$ with extrapolation weights $\mathbf{w}$, the unconstrained condition number is $\kappa_U = \|\mathbf{w}\|_1$.
This measures worst-case noise amplification: $|\hat{f}(0) - f(0)| \leq \kappa_U \cdot \max_i |\varepsilon_i|$.

Whether constrained estimators achieve $\kappa_B \leq \kappa_U$ remains an open question (see \cref{sec:limitations}).

\section{Non-Identifiability Without Assumptions}
\label{sec:nonident}

\begin{proposition}[Non-identifiability]
\label{prop:nonident}
Let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n > 0$ be distinct.
For any observations $y_1, \ldots, y_n \in \mathbb{R}$ and any target $t \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists a polynomial $p$ of degree at most $n$ such that $p(\lambda_i) = y_i$ for all $i$ and $p(0) = t$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The $n+1$ points $\{0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\}$ are distinct (since all $\lambda_i > 0$).
By the Lagrange interpolation theorem, there exists a unique polynomial of degree $\leq n$ taking any prescribed values at $n+1$ distinct points.
Explicitly:
\begin{equation}
p(x) = t \cdot L_0(x) + \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \cdot L_i(x)
\end{equation}
where $L_j(x) = \prod_{k \neq j} (x - x_k)/(x_j - x_k)$ are the Lagrange basis polynomials on $\{0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\}$.
Each $L_j$ has degree $n$, so $p$ has degree $\leq n$ and satisfies $p(0) = t$, $p(\lambda_i) = y_i$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:nonident}
For $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{P}_n$ and $\mathcal{C} = \mathbb{R}$: $\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{y}, \mathcal{P}_n, \mathbb{R}, 0) = \mathbb{R}$.
The ambiguity diameter is infinite; ZNE is completely undetermined.
\end{corollary}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
This is a negative identifiability result: without restricting the function class or imposing physical constraints, no finite amount of data can determine $f(0)$.
It does not claim that all practical ZNE fails---in practice, function classes are restricted (e.g., linear or quadratic models) and physical bounds apply.
The proposition motivates studying \emph{which} restrictions restore identifiability.

Two mechanisms reduce the ambiguity set:
(i) function-class restriction ($d < n$, creating an overdetermined system), and
(ii) physical constraints ($\mathcal{C} = [a,b]$, bounding the admissible range).
These are complementary: function-class restriction helps when the model is well-specified; physical constraints help unconditionally.

\section{Physical Admissibility via Spectral Constraints}
\label{sec:spectral}

\begin{proposition}[Spectral validity]
\label{prop:spectral}
Let $O$ be a Hermitian operator with eigenvalues $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_m$.
For any density operator $\rho$:
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\min} \leq \mathrm{Tr}[\rho O] \leq \mu_{\max}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In the eigenbasis of $O$: $\mathrm{Tr}[\rho O] = \sum_{j=1}^m \mu_j \langle e_j | \rho | e_j \rangle = \sum_j \mu_j p_j$,
where $p_j \geq 0$ (since $\rho \geq 0$) and $\sum_j p_j = 1$ (since $\mathrm{Tr}[\rho] = 1$).
A convex combination of real numbers lies within their range: $\mu_{\min} \leq \sum_j \mu_j p_j \leq \mu_{\max}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Projection reduces pointwise error]
\label{cor:proj}
For any scalar estimate $\hat{f}$ and true value $f(0) \in [\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]$:
\begin{equation}
|\mathrm{proj}_{[\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]}(\hat{f}) - f(0)| \leq |\hat{f} - f(0)|
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Projection onto a closed convex set $C$ is non-expansive: for any $x$ and $y \in C$, $|\mathrm{proj}_C(x) - y| \leq |x - y|$.
Since $f(0) \in [\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:spectral}, the result follows.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Important distinction.}
Corollary~\ref{cor:proj} applies to post-hoc clipping of a scalar estimate.
It does \emph{not} imply that constrained optimization (which changes all model parameters simultaneously) always reduces MSE.
Whether constrained optimization improves upon unconstrained estimation in general remains an open question.

\section{Ambiguity Diameter: Synthetic Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We compute the ambiguity diameter numerically by solving two linear programs (minimize and maximize $f(0)$) over the feasible set of polynomial coefficients consistent with data, tolerance, and bounds.

\subsection{Experimental setup}

All experiments use the synthetic response $f(\lambda) = 0.8 \cdot e^{-0.25\lambda}$ with true $f(0) = 0.8$.
Scale factors are evenly spaced from 1 to $n$.
The tolerance $\delta$ represents how closely the polynomial must pass through the data (an $\ell_\infty$ constraint), serving as a proxy for shot-noise uncertainty.
Three constraint sets are compared: no bounds $[-10, 10]$, Pauli $[-1, 1]$, and probability $[0, 1]$.

\subsection{Degree sensitivity}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/ambiguity_degree_sweep.pdf}
\caption{Ambiguity diameter vs polynomial degree for $n=5$, $\delta=0.01$.
At low degree, the overdetermined system constrains $f(0)$ regardless of bounds.
At degree $d = n-1 = 4$, bounds reduce ambiguity from 0.62 to 0.51 (18\%).}
\label{fig:degree_sweep}
\end{figure}

At degree 1, no linear function passes within $\delta = 0.01$ of the exponential data---the model is misspecified and the LP is infeasible.
At degrees 2--3, the overdetermined system constrains $f(0)$ tightly regardless of physical bounds.
At degree 4 (the interpolation regime, $d = n-1$), the polynomial class is flexible enough to admit multiple $f(0)$ values, and physical bounds provide the primary constraint (\cref{fig:degree_sweep}).

\subsection{Tolerance sensitivity}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/delta_sensitivity.pdf}
\caption{Ambiguity diameter vs tolerance $\delta$ for degree 4, $n=5$.
Without bounds, ambiguity grows linearly with $\delta$.
Probability bounds cap ambiguity at 1.0 regardless of $\delta$.}
\label{fig:delta}
\end{figure}

At $\delta = 0$ (exact data), the degree-4 polynomial is uniquely determined by 5 points, giving zero ambiguity.
As $\delta$ increases, the feasible set of polynomials grows.
At $\delta = 0.1$, probability bounds reduce ambiguity from 6.2 to 1.0---a $6.2\times$ reduction in this specific synthetic experiment (\cref{fig:delta}).
Physical bounds become increasingly important as measurement uncertainty grows.

\subsection{Sample-size sensitivity}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/n_sensitivity_interpolation_vs_fixed.pdf}
\caption{Ambiguity vs $n$ in two regimes.
Interpolation ($d = n-1$): ambiguity grows with $n$ because each new point adds a free parameter.
Fixed degree ($d = 2$): ambiguity shrinks as the system becomes increasingly overdetermined.}
\label{fig:n_sensitivity}
\end{figure}

In the interpolation regime ($d = n-1$), increasing $n$ does not reduce ambiguity because the polynomial degree grows with $n$---each new data point comes with a new free parameter.
The unbounded ambiguity approximately doubles across the tested range ($n = 3$ to 7).

In the fixed-degree regime ($d = 2$), adding data makes the system increasingly overdetermined.
Ambiguity shrinks from 0.14 ($n=3$) to 0.04 ($n=7$).

This illustrates that identifiability depends not on $n$ alone, but on the ratio $n/(d+1)$---how overdetermined the system is relative to model complexity (\cref{fig:n_sensitivity}).

\subsection{Bias--ambiguity tradeoff}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/bias_ambiguity_tradeoff.pdf}
\caption{Bias--ambiguity tradeoff for $n=7$, $\delta=0.01$, bounds $[0,1]$.
Lower degree reduces ambiguity but increases misspecification bias.
The optimal degree balances these competing objectives.}
\label{fig:tradeoff}
\end{figure}

We compute both the ambiguity diameter and the misspecification bias (noiseless least-squares estimate minus true $f(0)$) for each degree with $n = 7$:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
$d$ & $|\mathrm{Bias}|$ & Ambiguity \\
\midrule
2 & 0.035 & 0.044 \\
3 & 0.007 & 0.117 \\
4 & 0.001 & 0.244 \\
5 & 0.000 & 0.680 \\
6 & 0.000 & 2.540 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The tradeoff is clear (\cref{fig:tradeoff}): lower degree tightly identifies $f(0)$ but to a biased value (the polynomial cannot represent the exponential response); higher degree reduces bias but increases ambiguity.

\textbf{Implication.}
Identifiability alone is not accuracy.
A model can tightly identify the wrong value if misspecified.
Model selection is needed to balance bias against ambiguity, but the optimal degree depends on the unknown true response---the fundamental difficulty of model selection in ZNE.
We do not claim that AICc or any specific criterion optimally resolves this tradeoff.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The inverse-problem perspective on ZNE yields several insights:

\textbf{Physical bounds are most valuable in the high-uncertainty regime.}
When data is precise, the function class alone constrains $f(0)$.
When data is noisy (large $\delta$), physical bounds become the dominant---and sometimes only---constraint preventing unbounded ambiguity.

\textbf{The key ratio is $n/(d+1)$, not $n$ alone.}
More data helps only if model complexity is held fixed.
In the interpolation regime, adding measurements without constraining the model provides no identifiability benefit.

\textbf{Model selection is essential but fundamentally limited.}
The bias--ambiguity tradeoff shows that no single polynomial degree is universally optimal.
Information-theoretic criteria (AIC, AICc, BIC) offer principled approaches, but their optimality for ZNE in the small-sample regime ($n \leq 7$) is not established.

\textbf{Connection to QEM lower bounds.}
Takagi et al.~\cite{Takagi2022} and Quek et al.~\cite{Quek2024} establish exponential sampling overhead for generic QEM.
Our framework does not contradict these results.
Rather, it provides tools to study \emph{structured} settings where identifiability may be restored---an open research direction, not a claimed result.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Synthetic data only.} All results use analytically defined response functions. No quantum hardware or circuit simulation is involved.
\item \textbf{Polynomial function classes only.} Exponential and mixed models are not yet analyzed in the ambiguity framework.
\item \textbf{Single response function.} Most experiments use one exponential decay. Generalization to other response classes is future work.
\item \textbf{Tolerance chosen manually.} The connection between $\delta$ and actual shot-noise variance is noted but not rigorously derived.
\item \textbf{No hardware validation.} We make no claims about performance on real quantum processors.
\item \textbf{No proof that constrained optimization reduces MSE.} Propositions~\ref{prop:nonident}--\ref{prop:spectral} establish identifiability and validity, not accuracy of constrained estimators.
\item \textbf{No claim of defeating lower bounds.} We study structured identifiability, not violations of established complexity results.
\item \textbf{Some citations pending verification.} See source repository for details.
\end{itemize}
