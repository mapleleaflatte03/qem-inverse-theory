\begin{abstract}
We reframe zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) as a constrained inverse problem and study its identifiability, stability, and finite-shot behavior.
We prove that without function-class restrictions, the zero-noise limit is not identifiable from finite data (Proposition~\ref{prop:nonident}), and that spectral bounds from observable eigenvalues provide valid constraints whose projection cannot increase pointwise error (Proposition~\ref{prop:spectral}).
We introduce the \emph{ambiguity diameter} as a computable identifiability metric and demonstrate its dependence on model complexity, measurement tolerance, and physical bounds.

Beyond the foundational framework, we present a research program spanning five directions:
(i) regularized constrained estimators (Chebyshev--Tikhonov basis with physical bounds);
(ii) finite-shot help/harm phase diagrams identifying when mitigation helps versus harms;
(iii) bounded Bayesian ZNE with tanh output transform and coverage calibration;
(iv) sequential measurement design via posterior variance minimization;
(v) a structured escape-hatch taxonomy for QEM lower bounds.

We report both positive and negative findings.
Sequential design achieves 24\% MSE reduction in the ZNE-relevant regime ($\lambda \geq 1$).
Bayesian intervals are well-calibrated at low noise (89\% empirical for 90\% nominal) but under-cover at high noise (70\%).
A locality-aware regularization heuristic currently \emph{worsens} performance by 1--11\%, motivating calibrated alternatives.

All results are synthetic.
We do not claim hardware validation, universal estimator superiority, or circumvention of QEM lower bounds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) mitigates errors on noisy quantum processors by measuring expectation values at amplified noise levels and extrapolating to the zero-noise limit~\cite{LiBenjamin2017,Temme2017,GiurgicaTiron2020}.
Despite widespread use, ZNE is typically applied as ad hoc curve fitting without formal analysis of when the zero-noise limit can be reliably inferred.

We reframe ZNE as a \textbf{constrained inverse problem}~\cite{Hadamard1902} and ask: under what physical and statistical conditions is the zero-noise limit identifiable from finite noisy observations?
This perspective reveals that identifiability depends on the interaction between function class, physical constraints, measurement uncertainty, and problem structure.

Recent QEM lower bounds~\cite{Takagi2022,Quek2024} establish exponential sampling overhead for generic mitigation.
We do not claim to circumvent these results.
Instead, we study the mathematical structure of ZNE inference and identify candidate structural assumptions that may place specific instances outside the pessimistic generic regime.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item Constrained inverse-problem formulation with ambiguity diameter metric (\cref{sec:inverse}).
\item Non-identifiability proposition and spectral admissibility (\cref{sec:nonident,sec:spectral}).
\item Regularized estimators: bounded polynomial and Chebyshev--Tikhonov (\cref{sec:estimators}).
\item Finite-shot help/harm phase diagrams (\cref{sec:phase}).
\item Bounded Bayesian ZNE with calibration analysis (\cref{sec:bayesian}).
\item Sequential measurement design prototype (\cref{sec:sequential}).
\item Locality-aware prototype with documented negative result (\cref{sec:locality}).
\item Structured escape-hatch taxonomy (\cref{sec:taxonomy}).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Related work.}
Miranskyy et al.~\cite{Miranskyy2026} proposed physically bounded ZNE models with empirical benchmarking (arXiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed).
Our work is complementary: we study the theoretical structure underlying such bounds rather than proposing a production estimator.

\section{ZNE as a Constrained Inverse Problem}
\label{sec:inverse}

The observation model is $y_i = f(\lambda_i) + \varepsilon_i$ for $i=1,\ldots,n$, where $\lambda_i > 0$ are fixed scale factors and $f(0)$ is the extrapolation target.

\begin{definition}[Ambiguity set]
$\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{y}, \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{C}, \delta) = \{ f(0) \mid f \in \mathcal{F},\, f(0) \in \mathcal{C},\, \|f(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) - \mathbf{y}\|_\infty \leq \delta \}$
\end{definition}

The ambiguity diameter $\mathrm{diam}(\mathcal{A})$ measures identifiability: zero diameter means $f(0)$ is uniquely determined.

\section{Non-Identifiability Without Assumptions}
\label{sec:nonident}

\begin{proposition}[Non-identifiability]
\label{prop:nonident}
For any $n$ observations and any target $t \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists a degree-$n$ polynomial matching the data with $p(0) = t$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Lagrange interpolation on $n+1$ distinct points $\{0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\}$.
\end{proof}

\section{Physical Admissibility}
\label{sec:spectral}

\begin{proposition}[Spectral validity]
\label{prop:spectral}
For Hermitian $O$ with eigenvalues $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_m$ and any state $\rho$: $\mu_{\min} \leq \mathrm{Tr}[\rho O] \leq \mu_{\max}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{Tr}[\rho O] = \sum_j \mu_j p_j$ with $p_j \geq 0$, $\sum p_j = 1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
Post-hoc projection onto $[\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]$ cannot increase pointwise error.
\end{corollary}

This does \emph{not} imply constrained optimization always reduces MSE.

\section{Regularized Constrained Estimators}
\label{sec:estimators}

We implement two constrained estimators:
(i) bounded polynomial via L-BFGS-B with $f(0) \in [a,b]$;
(ii) Chebyshev--Tikhonov: Chebyshev basis expansion with Tikhonov penalty $\lambda \|\mathbf{c}\|^2$ and SLSQP-enforced bounds.

The Chebyshev basis maps observed scales to $[-1,1]$; the extrapolation point $\lambda=0$ maps to $z_0 < -1$ (genuine extrapolation outside the basis domain).
A condition-number proxy $\kappa = \|\mathbf{e}_0^\top \Phi^+\|_1$ quantifies noise amplification.

\section{Bayesian Bounded ZNE and Calibration}
\label{sec:bayesian}

We implement a GP on latent $g(\lambda)$ with $f(\lambda) = \mathrm{mid} + r \cdot \tanh(g(\lambda))$, guaranteeing $f \in (a,b)$.
Posterior at $\lambda=0$ is propagated via the delta method.

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/bayesian_coverage.pdf}
\caption{Bayesian credible interval calibration.
At low noise (0.10), 90\% intervals achieve 89\% empirical coverage.
At high noise (0.25), coverage drops to 70\%---a calibration limitation of fixed hyperparameters, not a fundamental failure.}
\label{fig:coverage}
\end{figure}

Marginal likelihood optimization improves NLL but does not fully resolve high-noise undercoverage.
This is an honest negative finding: the method needs further calibration work.

\section{Sequential Measurement Design}
\label{sec:sequential}

We implement greedy one-step lookahead: select the next scale factor minimizing posterior variance at $\lambda=0$.

With candidates restricted to $\lambda \geq 1$ (standard ZNE regime), sequential design achieves 24.3\% MSE reduction versus static 5-point design in the synthetic exponential setup.
The algorithm selects $\lambda = 1.0$ and $\lambda = 1.75$---low-noise points most informative for extrapolation.

Allowing $\lambda < 1$ (non-standard, synthetic only) gives 66\% improvement but is not applicable to hardware ZNE where noise is amplified ($\lambda \geq 1$).

\section{Locality-Aware Prototype: A Negative Result}
\label{sec:locality}

We test whether observable support size can guide regularization strength.
A heuristic maps support size to Tikhonov $\lambda$ via a locality envelope proxy.

\textbf{Result:} The heuristic \emph{worsens} MSE by 1--11\% across all tested observables.
The recommended $\lambda \approx 0.07$ is $\sim$70$\times$ larger than the fixed baseline (0.001), causing over-regularization.

This motivates calibrated locality mappings or commutator-derived envelopes (future work).
It does not mean locality-aware ZNE is impossible---only that the current heuristic is too aggressive.

\section{Structured Escape-Hatch Taxonomy}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

Generic QEM lower bounds apply to worst-case circuits.
We identify six candidate structural assumptions that may place specific instances outside the pessimistic regime:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Structure & Possible benefit \\
\midrule
Low support ($k \ll n$) & Effective dimension $\sim k$ \\
Low depth & Smoother noise response \\
Symmetry & Reduced Hilbert space \\
Weak scrambling & Structured extrapolation \\
Smooth response class & Constrained ambiguity \\
Bayesian abstention & Avoid harmful mitigation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

These are \textbf{research directions, not results}.
No theorem proves any structure achieves polynomial-cost mitigation.

\section{Limitations and Non-Claims}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item All results are synthetic. No quantum hardware is involved.
\item We do not claim any estimator is universally superior.
\item We do not defeat, circumvent, or overcome QEM lower bounds.
\item The locality-aware heuristic currently harms performance.
\item Bayesian intervals are not calibrated at high noise.
\item Sequential design is greedy one-step, not globally optimal.
\item The escape-hatch taxonomy is not theorem-backed.
\item Some citations are pending final verification.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented ZNE as a constrained inverse problem and developed a research program spanning identifiability theory, regularized estimation, Bayesian uncertainty quantification, sequential design, and structured escape-hatch analysis.

The key insight is that identifiability---whether $f(0)$ can be reliably inferred---depends on the interaction between function class, physical constraints, and measurement budget, not on any single estimator choice.

We report both successes (ambiguity reduction via bounds, 24\% MSE improvement from sequential design, calibrated Bayesian intervals at low noise) and failures (locality heuristic over-regularizes, Bayesian undercoverage at high noise).
Both types of findings advance understanding of when and why ZNE works.

Future work includes rigorous risk bounds, commutator-derived locality envelopes, circuit-family benchmarks, and hardware validation.
