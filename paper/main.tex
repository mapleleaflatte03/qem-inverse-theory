\begin{abstract}
We reframe zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) as a constrained inverse problem---recovering an unobserved noiseless expectation from finite noisy measurements---and introduce the \emph{ambiguity diameter}, a computable metric quantifying how tightly the zero-noise limit is determined by data, function class, and physical constraints.
We show via elementary interpolation that without function-class restrictions, the zero-noise limit is not identifiable (Proposition~\ref{prop:nonident}), and that spectral projection cannot increase pointwise error (Proposition~\ref{prop:spectral}).
Synthetic experiments illustrate that physical bounds reduce ambiguity by up to $6.2\times$ at high tolerance, that identifiability depends on the overdetermination ratio $n/(d+1)$, and that a bias--ambiguity tradeoff governs model selection.
We further explore finite-shot help/harm phase diagrams, bounded Bayesian ZNE with coverage calibration, sequential measurement design (24\% MSE reduction in one synthetic setup with $\lambda \geq 1$), and a locality-aware prototype that currently worsens performance (negative result).
All results are synthetic; we do not claim hardware validation, universal superiority, or circumvention of QEM lower bounds~\cite{Takagi2022,Quek2024}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE) mitigates errors on noisy quantum processors by measuring expectation values at amplified noise levels and extrapolating to the zero-noise limit~\cite{LiBenjamin2017,Temme2017,GiurgicaTiron2020}.
Despite widespread use, ZNE is typically applied as ad hoc curve fitting without formal analysis of when the zero-noise limit can be reliably inferred.

We reframe ZNE as a \textbf{constrained inverse problem} and ask: under what conditions is the zero-noise limit identifiable from finite noisy observations?
In the sense of Hadamard~\cite{Hadamard1902}, an inverse problem is well-posed if its solution exists, is unique, and depends continuously on the data.
ZNE generically fails uniqueness and stability; physical constraints and function-class restrictions are the tools available to restore partial well-posedness.

This is a \textbf{framework paper with supporting synthetic experiments}.
We develop diagnostic tools (ambiguity diameter, help/harm phase diagrams, coverage calibration) and explore five directions, reporting both positive and negative findings.
We do not propose a production estimator or claim to circumvent QEM lower bounds~\cite{Takagi2022,Quek2024}.

\textbf{Contributions:}
(1) ambiguity diameter as identifiability metric;
(2) non-identifiability and spectral admissibility propositions;
(3) regularized constrained estimators;
(4) finite-shot help/harm diagnostics;
(5) bounded Bayesian ZNE with calibration;
(6) sequential design prototype;
(7) locality-aware negative result;
(8) structured escape-hatch taxonomy.

\textbf{Related work.}
Miranskyy et al.~\cite{Miranskyy2026} proposed physically bounded ZNE models with empirical benchmarking across 180,000 circuits (arXiv preprint, not yet peer-reviewed).
Their contribution is an \emph{estimator}: a bounded curve-fitting method that prevents unphysical predictions.
Our contribution is an \emph{analysis of the inference problem itself}: we study when and why the zero-noise limit is identifiable, how ambiguity depends on model complexity and constraints, and where mitigation helps versus harms.
These are complementary perspectives---one engineering, one mathematical---and neither subsumes the other.

\section{Framework: ZNE as Constrained Inverse Problem}
\label{sec:inverse}

\subsection{Observation model and design}

The observation model is $y_i = f(\lambda_i) + \varepsilon_i$ for $i=1,\ldots,n$, where $\lambda_i > 0$ are fixed, known scale factors (chosen by the experimenter via unitary folding), $f(\lambda) = \mathrm{Tr}[\mathcal{E}_\lambda(\rho) O]$ is the noise response, and $\varepsilon_i$ is shot noise with variance $\sigma_i^2$.
The target $f(0)$ is never observed.

\subsection{Function class and constraints}

\begin{definition}[Function class]
$\mathcal{F}$ is the set of admissible response functions (e.g., polynomials $\mathcal{P}_d$ of degree $\leq d$).
The choice of $\mathcal{F}$ is an assumption, not a consequence of data.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Physical constraint set]
For observable $O$ with eigenvalues $\{\mu_j\}$: $\mathcal{C} = [\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Ambiguity set and diameter}

\begin{definition}[Ambiguity set]
Given tolerance $\delta \geq 0$ (representing measurement uncertainty):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}(\mathbf{y}, \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{C}, \delta) = \left\{ f(0) \;\middle|\; f \in \mathcal{F},\, f(0) \in \mathcal{C},\, \|f(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) - \mathbf{y}\|_\infty \leq \delta \right\}
\end{equation}
The \textbf{ambiguity diameter} $\mathrm{diam}(\mathcal{A}) = \sup\mathcal{A} - \inf\mathcal{A}$ measures identifiability.
\end{definition}

We compute $\mathrm{diam}(\mathcal{A})$ via linear programming (minimize/maximize $f(0)$ over feasible polynomial coefficients).

\section{Identifiability Results}
\label{sec:nonident}

\begin{proposition}[Non-identifiability]
\label{prop:nonident}
For any $n$ observations at distinct $\lambda_i > 0$ and any target $t \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists a degree-$n$ polynomial with $p(\lambda_i) = y_i$ and $p(0) = t$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Lagrange interpolation on $\{0, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\}$ ($n+1$ distinct points).
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation:} Without restricting $\mathcal{F}$ or imposing $\mathcal{C}$, ZNE is undetermined.
This motivates studying which restrictions restore identifiability.
It does not claim practical ZNE always fails---in practice, low-degree models and physical bounds apply.

\begin{proposition}[Spectral validity]
\label{prop:spectral}
For Hermitian $O$ with eigenvalues $\mu_1,\ldots,\mu_m$ and any state $\rho$: $\mu_{\min} \leq \mathrm{Tr}[\rho O] \leq \mu_{\max}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}
Post-hoc projection onto $[\mu_{\min}, \mu_{\max}]$ cannot increase pointwise error when the true value lies within bounds.
This does \emph{not} imply constrained optimization always reduces MSE.
\end{corollary}

We implement bounded polynomial (L-BFGS-B) and Chebyshev--Tikhonov (SLSQP with penalty $\lambda\|\mathbf{c}\|^2$) estimators.

For the exponential response (\cref{fig:phase}, \cref{tab:methods}), polynomial-based methods help in 16/20 cells.
Similar patterns hold for quadratic and mixed-oscillatory responses (e.g., Chebyshev--Tikhonov achieves MSE 0.002 vs raw 0.010 for the quadratic response at noise${}=0.1$, shots${}=1000$), though rankings can differ across response families.

Other response functions may yield different rankings.

The following proposition formalizes the help/harm boundary under a stylized bias--variance model.

\begin{proposition}[Finite-shot help/harm threshold]
\label{prop:helpharm}
Let the raw estimator have $\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{raw}} = B_{\mathrm{raw}}^2 + V_{\mathrm{raw}}/N$ and a ZNE estimator have $\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{ZNE}} = B_{\mathrm{ZNE}}^2 + V_{\mathrm{ZNE}}/N$, where $B^2$ is squared bias, $V/N$ is variance (with $N$ total shots), and both depend on the response function and method.
Then ZNE improves over raw ($\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{ZNE}} < \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{raw}}$) if and only if:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:helpharm}
\Delta B^2 > \frac{\Delta V}{N}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta B^2 = B_{\mathrm{raw}}^2 - B_{\mathrm{ZNE}}^2$ (bias reduction) and $\Delta V = V_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - V_{\mathrm{raw}}$ (variance amplification).
If $\Delta B^2 > 0$ and $\Delta V > 0$, the critical shot count is $N^* = \Delta V / \Delta B^2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{ZNE}} < \mathrm{MSE}_{\mathrm{raw}}$ iff $B_{\mathrm{ZNE}}^2 + V_{\mathrm{ZNE}}/N < B_{\mathrm{raw}}^2 + V_{\mathrm{raw}}/N$, which rearranges to $(B_{\mathrm{raw}}^2 - B_{\mathrm{ZNE}}^2) > (V_{\mathrm{ZNE}} - V_{\mathrm{raw}})/N$.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Interpretation.}
This is a stylized criterion, not a universal theorem.
It assumes bias and variance are known and decomposable, which they are not in practice.
However, it explains the phase-diagram structure: at low $N$, variance amplification dominates ($\Delta V/N$ is large) and ZNE harms; at high $N$, bias reduction dominates and ZNE helps.
The threshold $N^*$ depends on the specific response and method---it is not a universal constant.

\section{Locality-Aware Prototype: Negative Result}
\label{sec:locality}

A heuristic mapping support size to Tikhonov $\lambda$ via a locality envelope proxy \emph{worsens} MSE by 1--11\%.
The recommended $\lambda \approx 0.07$ over-regularizes relative to the baseline (0.001).
This motivates calibrated mappings or commutator-derived envelopes, but does not mean locality-aware ZNE is impossible.

\section{Limitations, Non-Claims, and Future Directions}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Limitations:}
All results are synthetic.
We do not claim universal superiority, hardware validation, or circumvention of QEM lower bounds.
The locality heuristic harms performance.
Bayesian intervals are uncalibrated at high noise.
Sequential design is greedy, not optimal.

\textbf{Structured escape hatches (future directions):}
We identify six candidate structures (low support, low depth, symmetry, weak scrambling, smooth response, Bayesian abstention) that may place instances outside generic lower-bound pessimism.
These are research directions, not results; no theorem proves any achieves polynomial-cost mitigation.

\textbf{Reproducibility:}
All code, experiments, and figures are available at the source repository with 80 automated tests.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Our analysis suggests that ZNE identifiability depends on the interaction between function class, physical constraints, and measurement budget.
We report both successes (ambiguity reduction, 24\% sequential improvement, calibrated Bayesian intervals at low noise) and failures (locality over-regularization, Bayesian undercoverage at high noise).
Both advance understanding of when ZNE works.
Future work includes rigorous risk bounds, commutator-derived envelopes, circuit-family benchmarks, and eventual hardware evaluation.
